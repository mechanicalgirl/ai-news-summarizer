### XOR'ing a register with itself is the idiom for zeroing it out. Why not sub? - (Hacker News)

https://devblogs.microsoft.com/oldnewthing/20260421-00/?p=112247

# Summary

This article discusses why the `xor` instruction became the standard way to zero out a register in x86 assembly, even though `sub` is equally efficient in terms of size and execution cycles. While `sub` actually has slightly better flag behavior (it clears the AF flag whereas `xor` leaves it undefined), `xor` gained dominance through what the author calls "swarming"—an initial slight preference that snowballed as compilers adopted it, leading programmers to assume compiler writers knew something they didn't.

The adoption of `xor` became so widespread that Intel and other CPU manufacturers eventually added special hardware detection to recognize the `xor r, r` pattern and bypass execution entirely by using an internal zero register. While some competing processors may have only optimized for `xor` and not `sub`, this technical advantage reinforced `xor`'s position as the standard idiom. The article illustrates how minor historical contingencies can become entrenched conventions in programming, even when technically equivalent alternatives exist.

### Acetaminophen vs. ibuprofen - (Hacker News)

https://asteriskmag.com/issues/14/the-mystery-in-the-medicine-cabinet

# Summary

This article explores why acetaminophen is often safer than ibuprofen for most people, contrary to common assumptions. The author explains that while acetaminophen has a narrow therapeutic window and can cause liver failure at high doses, ibuprofen poses broader systemic risks by inhibiting enzymes throughout the body, affecting the stomach, heart, and kidneys. Acetaminophen's effects are largely confined to the central nervous system, making it safer at recommended doses despite its more dramatic potential for overdose toxicity.

The article reveals why this crucial safety distinction isn't common knowledge: understanding it requires deep knowledge of metabolic pathways and drug mechanisms. Even people with liver disease should typically avoid ibuprofen and use lower-dose acetaminophen instead, since ibuprofen's kidney effects are particularly dangerous for that population. The author concludes that drug safety information should be more accessible and transparent on labels and health websites rather than remaining illegible to the general public.

### Diverse organic molecules on Mars revealed by the first SAM TMAH experiment - (Hacker News)

https://www.courthousenews.com/preserved-for-billions-of-years-organic-compounds-found-on-mars/

# Summary

NASA's Curiosity rover has discovered over 20 organic compounds preserved in Martian rock samples from Gale Crater, including a nitrogen-bearing molecule similar to DNA building blocks—a first for Mars. The findings, published in Nature Communications, demonstrate that Mars can preserve complex organic material for billions of years, suggesting the planet may have once had conditions suitable for life. However, the organic molecules alone do not prove that life ever existed on Mars, as these compounds can form through non-biological processes or arrive via meteorites.

The discovery was made using a chemical analysis technique called TMAH on samples collected from the Glen Torridon region in 2020. While the results don't confirm past Martian life, they show that preserved organic compounds capable of indicating life's existence can survive in Mars's shallow subsurface, which has important implications for future missions searching for evidence of life on Mars and Saturn's moon Titan.

### Garbage Collection Without Unsafe Code - (Hacker News)

https://fitzgen.com/2024/02/06/safe-gc.html

# Summary

Nick Fitzgerald presents **safe-gc**, a garbage collection library for Rust that contains zero unsafe code—neither in its API nor implementation. While most GC libraries in Rust require unsafe code internally, Fitzgerald demonstrates that it's possible to build a fully safe GC system by using heap indexing rather than direct pointer dereferencing. Users define GC-managed types with `Gc<T>` references and implement the `Trace` trait (which is now safe to implement) to report GC edges to the collector. Objects are allocated in a `Heap` and accessed via indexing rather than dereferencing, which enables the safe implementation while maintaining Rust's ownership and borrowing discipline.

The library uses a simple arena-based storage system with typed arenas backed by `Vec` and free lists that manage indices rather than raw memory, eliminating the need for unsafe pointer arithmetic. It provides two reference types: `Gc<T>` (Copy, for inter-object references) and `Root<T>` (non-Copy, for holding references across GC operations). While safe-gc prioritizes safety over performance, Fitzgerald's work serves as proof of concept that unsafe code is not strictly necessary for implementing garbage collection in Rust.

### Windows Server 2025 Runs Better on ARM - (Hacker News)

https://jasoneckert.github.io/myblog/server-2025-arm64/

# Summary

Jason Eckert, while updating his Windows Server textbook, discovered that Windows Server 2025 runs noticeably faster on ARM architecture (Snapdragon X Elite) than on Intel's x64 architecture, despite both systems being identically configured. Through performance testing, he found that ARM's steady, consistent performance delivery is better suited to typical Windows Server workloads—which involve frequent small operations sensitive to latency—compared to Intel's dynamic boost/throttle approach that introduces scheduling variability, especially problematic in virtualized environments.

Eckert argues this suggests Microsoft should expand ARM64 support for Windows Server, given that ARM already comprises a significant portion of cloud deployments and shows performance advantages for latency-sensitive workloads. However, he notes that for his textbook, x64 remains the practical choice due to limitations with nested virtualization on ARM64 in Hyper-V.
