### GM agrees to pay $12.75M in California driver privacy settlement - (TechCrunch)

https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/09/gm-agrees-to-pay-12-75m-in-california-driver-privacy-settlement/

# Summary

General Motors has agreed to pay $12.75 million to settle privacy violations with California's Attorney General, stemming from the company's unauthorized sale of driving behavior data from hundreds of thousands of Californians to data brokers Verisk Analytics and LexisNexis Risk Solutions. The data, collected through GM's OnStar program, generated approximately $20 million in sales for the automaker. While California's insurance laws prevented the data from actually increasing insurance rates in the state, the settlement requires GM to cease selling driving data to consumer reporting agencies for five years and delete retained customer data within 180 days unless explicit consent is obtained.

This settlement follows a previous Federal Trade Commission enforcement action against GM and reinforces ongoing regulatory scrutiny of automakers' data practices. GM indicated that the agreement addresses its discontinued "Smart Driver" product and reflects strengthened privacy measures. The case underscores California's data minimization principle, which prohibits companies from retaining data beyond its original purpose without consumer knowledge and consent.

### Pushing Local Models With Focus And Polish - (Armin Ronacher's Thoughts and Writings)

https://lucumr.pocoo.org/2026/5/8/local-models/

# Summary

Armin Ronacher argues that while local AI models have improved technically, they remain difficult to use in practice compared to hosted APIs. The problem isn't model quality but rather fragmentation across numerous inference engines, inconsistent implementations (tool parameter streaming, context handling, quantization choices), and lack of polish. He advocates for focused, end-to-end optimization of specific model-hardware combinations rather than spreading effort across countless generic solutions.

To demonstrate this approach, Ronacher highlights ds4.c, a deliberately narrow inference engine built specifically for DeepSeek V4 Flash on high-end Macs, and his pi-ds4 extension that integrates it seamlessly into the Pi coding agent with zero configuration. His goal is to answer whether local models can achieve hosted API-like ergonomics when treated as first-class products rather than manual configurations, and to prove that focused effort on one winning combination can create a superior experience while keeping powerful tools accessible outside of corporate data centers.

### Content for Content’s Sake - (Armin Ronacher's Thoughts and Writings)

https://lucumr.pocoo.org/2026/5/4/content-for-contents-sake/

# Summary

Armin Ronacher examines how large language models (LLMs) are subtly infiltrating human communication and online spaces. He presents evidence that certain words appear with unusual frequency in his coding sessions—correlating with Google Trends spikes—suggesting LLM influence on language use. Beyond vocabulary, he notes that LLM-generated text's characteristic sentence structures are increasingly bleeding into human writing, making it difficult to distinguish authentic human discourse from AI-generated content. This phenomenon extends across platforms like Twitter, LinkedIn, and GitHub, where he observes proliferation of AI-generated posts, comments, and projects.

The core problem, Ronacher argues, is that LLM-generated "slop" spreads rapidly and performs well algorithmically despite requiring minimal effort, creating an arms race that drowns out genuine human signal. He contends that low-friction content creation should be slowed through friction and rate-limiting mechanisms, similar to how societies have restricted other high-risk, easily-exploitable systems. Ultimately, Ronacher expresses concern about erosion of trust in human interactions—as people increasingly adopt LLM phrasing patterns, he finds himself doubting whether he's communicating with actual humans, a psychological toll that technology alone cannot solve.
