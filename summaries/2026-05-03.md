### AI-generated actors and scripts are now ineligible for Oscars - (TechCrunch)

https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/02/ai-generated-actors-and-scripts-are-now-ineligible-for-oscars/

# Summary

The Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences has announced new Oscar eligibility rules that prohibit AI-generated actors and scripts from competing. According to the guidelines, only performances "demonstrably performed by humans with their consent" and "human-authored" screenplays qualify for Academy Awards. The academy also reserves the right to request information about a film's AI usage and human authorship.

These rule changes reflect growing concerns within the entertainment industry about artificial intelligence, particularly following high-profile cases like an independent film using an AI-generated Val Kilmer and the AI "actress" Tilly Norwood. The decision aligns with ongoing industry efforts to protect human creators, as AI was a major issue during the 2023 actors' and writers' strikes, and similar restrictions are being adopted across other creative fields.

### Farewell, Jeeves: Ask.com shuts down - (TechCrunch)

https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/02/farewell-jeeves-ask-com-shuts-down/

# Summary

Ask.com, the search engine and Q&A service formerly known as Ask Jeeves, has officially shut down as of May 1, 2026. The service, which launched in 1996 and pioneered natural language question-answering (predating modern AI chatbots), had been acquired by holding company IAC in 2005. Despite its innovative approach, Ask.com was consistently overshadowed by Google and struggled to remain competitive in the search market.

IAC, which owns the service, decided to discontinue Ask.com as part of a broader strategic refocus on other business priorities. The company's announcement noted that after 25 years of operation, Ask.com has closed, though it assured users that "Jeeves' spirit endures." The shutdown reflects the challenging reality that Ask.com never managed to carve out a significant market share against dominant search competitors.

### Uber wants to turn its millions of drivers into a sensor grid for self-driving companies - (TechCrunch)

https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/01/uber-wants-to-turn-its-millions-of-drivers-into-a-sensor-grid-for-self-driving-companies/

# Summary

Uber is developing an ambitious plan to transform its millions of drivers' vehicles into a data-collection network for autonomous vehicle (AV) companies. Through its AV Labs program, Uber aims to eventually equip human drivers' cars with sensors to gather real-world data that AV companies can use to train their models. Currently operating a small dedicated fleet of sensor-equipped vehicles, Uber plans to scale this into a massive "AV cloud"—a labeled data library that partner companies (currently 25 AV firms) can access and use for model training and testing.

This strategic pivot represents Uber's answer to remaining relevant in an autonomous vehicle-dominated future, particularly after abandoning its own self-driving car ambitions years ago. By positioning itself as the data layer for the entire AV ecosystem, Uber could leverage its unparalleled access to real-world driving scenarios and millions of vehicles to become indispensable to the autonomous vehicle industry. While regulatory hurdles and clarifications remain, this move addresses what Uber's CTO identifies as the true bottleneck in AV development: access to diverse, large-scale training data.

### Before GitHub - (Armin Ronacher's Thoughts and Writings)

https://lucumr.pocoo.org/2026/4/28/before-github/

# Summary

Armin Ronacher reflects on GitHub's evolution and current decline, tracing how it became central to open-source development after earlier platforms like SourceForge and his own self-hosted infrastructure. GitHub revolutionized open source by making project creation, discovery, and contribution frictionless, while also serving as an archival system that preserved countless projects and discussions. However, this centralization created an ironic paradox: distributed version control systems enabled decentralization, yet the world standardized on one massive centralized service.

The author argues that GitHub's decline matters beyond just product decisions, as it was woven into open source's social fabric and trust infrastructure. He notes that the explosion of micro-dependencies and npm packages relied on GitHub's implicit trustworthiness, which is now eroding due to instability, leadership issues, and feature creep. Recent high-profile migrations (like Ghostty) signal that developers are increasingly exploring alternatives to GitHub, suggesting a potential shift away from the platform's dominance—which Ronacher sees as both necessary and historically inevitable.
