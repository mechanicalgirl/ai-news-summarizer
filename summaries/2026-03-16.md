### Canada's bill C-22 mandates mass metadata surveillance - (Hacker News)

https://www.michaelgeist.ca/2026/03/a-tale-of-two-bills-lawful-access-returns-with-changes-to-warrantless-access-but-dangerous-backdoor-surveillance-risks-remains/

# Summary

Canada's government has introduced Bill C-22, the Lawful Access Act, marking a new phase in the decades-long debate over law enforcement access to personal information. The bill comprises two main components: one addressing law enforcement access to data held by telecom and internet providers, and another (SAAIA) establishing surveillance capabilities within Canadian networks. The first component represents a significant improvement over the previous Bill C-2 attempt, narrowing warrantless access demands to only confirming whether telecom providers serve particular individuals, with access to additional subscriber information now requiring judicial approval through production orders. However, this improvement comes at a cost.

The concerning aspect of Bill C-22 lies in its SAAIA provisions, which largely remain unchanged from the problematic Bill C-2 and even expand certain obligations, particularly requiring "core providers" (including major internet platforms like Google and Meta) to retain metadata for up to one year. The bill exposes serious privacy and security risks by requiring providers to assist government access to their networks for surveillance testing, while keeping these activities secret. Critics worry the secrecy surrounding these obligations, combined with global data-sharing implications and insufficient protections against creating systemic network vulnerabilities, poses substantial risks to privacy and civil liberties despite the government's claimed oversight improvements through Intelligence Commissioner approval.

### The 49MB web page - (Hacker News)

https://thatshubham.com/blog/news-audit

# Summary

Modern news websites have become bloated and deliberately hostile to readers. A typical New York Times article load consumes 49MB of data—equivalent to Windows 95 or a dozen MP3 songs—through 422 network requests, largely due to programmatic ad auctions, tracking scripts, and surveillance infrastructure running in the browser. This creates performance problems, drains device batteries, and violates user privacy, all while cookie consent banners provide mere legal cover for data mining operations.

Publishers prioritize these exploitative practices because the ad-tech industry incentivizes metrics like time-on-page and viewability through higher CPM rates, forcing designers to implement deliberately user-hostile patterns. Common hostile UX tactics include overlapping modals, viewport-blocking ads, content-obscuring pop-ups, sudden layout shifts from late-loading ads, and sticky autoplaying videos—all designed to increase engagement metrics at the expense of user experience. These anti-patterns violate established usability principles and Google's own Core Web Vitals guidelines, yet persist because media companies feel trapped in a "programmatic ad-tech death spiral" and domain authority keeps them ranked despite poor UX.

### Chrome DevTools MCP (2025) - (Hacker News)

https://developer.chrome.com/blog/chrome-devtools-mcp-debug-your-browser-session

# Summary

Chrome DevTools has introduced a new Model Context Protocol (MCP) enhancement that allows coding agents to directly connect to active browser sessions, eliminating the need for separate sign-ins or isolated browsing contexts. The feature enables agents to access existing browser sessions and active debugging data from DevTools panels (such as selected network requests or DOM elements), creating a seamless workflow where developers can manually debug issues and then hand off investigation tasks to AI agents.

The new auto-connection feature, available in Chrome M144 (Beta), requires users to explicitly enable remote debugging at chrome://inspect/#remote-debugging and configure the Chrome DevTools MCP server with the --autoConnect flag. Security is maintained through permission dialogs that require explicit user approval each time an agent requests a debugging session, and Chrome displays a banner indicating automated control is active.

### Electric motor scaling laws and inertia in robot actuators - (Hacker News)

https://robot-daycare.com/posts/actuation_series_1/

# Summary

This article explores fundamental scaling laws for electric motors and their implications for robot actuators. The author derives that motor torque, mass, power dissipation, and rotor inertia scale differently depending on whether motor length or radius changes, then introduces a size-invariant "figure of merit" (FoM) that describes how much force a motor can produce relative to its power dissipation and mass. Using real-world data from frameless motor catalogs, the author shows this FoM remains relatively constant across motors of vastly different sizes (16 grams to 1 kg), validating the theoretical predictions.

The key insight is that for actuators producing the same output torque with the same power dissipation, the reflected inertia is essentially independent of gear ratio and motor size—a counterintuitive result where smaller motors with higher gear ratios perform comparably to larger direct-drive motors. However, the author acknowledges important limitations: the FoM doesn't account for peak torque saturation, heat transfer challenges (smaller motors have less surface area to dissipate the same power), or absolute rotor inertia differences within motor families. The analysis applies broadly across motor topologies, though certain designs like transverse flux motors operate under different constraints.

### LLMs can be exhausting - (Hacker News)

https://tomjohnell.com/llms-can-be-absolutely-exhausting/

# Summary

Tom Johnell describes the mental exhaustion that comes from extended sessions with large language models (LLMs) like Claude and Codex, arguing that the problem often lies not with the AI itself but with the user's approach. He identifies two main culprits: mental fatigue leading to lower-quality prompts and interruptions, and slow feedback loops that bloat the model's context window, degrading its performance over time.

The author advocates for a more deliberate, test-driven approach to working with LLMs. He recommends taking breaks when losing enjoyment in prompt writing, ensuring clarity and confidence in prompts before submitting them, and most importantly, optimizing feedback loops by setting clear success criteria and working with the AI to reproduce failures quickly. By treating LLM interactions more like engineering problems with explicit constraints, he found he could iterate faster, consume less context, and ultimately save hours of debugging time.

### How far can you go with IX Route Servers only? - (Hacker News)

https://blog.benjojo.co.uk/post/how-far-can-you-get-with-ix-route-servers

# Summary

This article examines the viability of building an internet network using only IX (Internet Exchange) Route Servers without transit or bilateral peering. The author found that while route servers provide excellent outbound reachability—covering roughly 56-61% of the internet's routing table across 100+ exchanges—the inbound reachability is surprisingly low at only around 14%. This discrepancy occurs because most networks export routes to route servers but don't import them, creating an asymmetry in traffic flow capabilities.

The conclusion is that route servers are effective for networks with outbound-heavy traffic patterns, allowing access to diverse peering with minimal configuration effort. However, for inbound-heavy networks (like residential ISPs), route servers prove ineffective as a traffic attraction strategy. The author notes that most inbound traffic is concentrated among five major content networks (Meta, Akamai, Google, Netflix, Amazon), making direct Private Network Interconnects more economical than relying on IX route servers.
