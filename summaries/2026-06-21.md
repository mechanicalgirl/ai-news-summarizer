### In the Weights is your new AI-centric vanity search - (TechCrunch)

https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/20/in-the-weights-is-your-new-ai-centric-vanity-search/

# Summary

In the Weights is a new website created by former OpenAI designers Thomas Dimson and Joey Flynn that measures how well people are "remembered" by AI models rather than through traditional web search. The site queries multiple AI models (including GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama, and Grok) about individuals, clusters their responses, and assigns a "strength score" based on how consistently the models can recall information about a person without using web search tools. The creators argue this reflects a shift in how people learn about others, as AI chatbots increasingly compete with Google search as primary information sources.

The website has gained unexpected traction with users, who find the comparative strength scores both intriguing and competitive. The rankings feature a playful, retro Nintendo-inspired design and include prominent figures like actor Macaulay Culkin at the top of the leaderboard. Dimson plans to further investigate why different AI models return varying results, potential biases in model training, and which individuals deserve Wikipedia articles but lack them, highlighting broader questions about who and what gets encoded into AI systems' "weights."

### From PGP to Mythos: a brief history of export controls that didn’t stop anyone - (TechCrunch)

https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/19/encryption-spyware-and-now-mythos-history-shows-why-cyber-export-control-doesnt-work/

# Summary

The U.S. White House recently ordered Anthropic to restrict exports of its AI models Fable and Mythos, citing national security concerns, after the company granted a South Korean telecom access to Mythos and researchers found potential safety workarounds. This marks the first major test of whether export controls can effectively contain frontier AI technology, similar to past attempts with encryption and spyware.

However, the article highlights that government export controls have a poor historical track record of achieving their goals. Examples include the failed PGP encryption restrictions of the 1990s (which ultimately led to widespread encryption adoption) and the largely ineffective Wassenaar Arrangement for controlling spyware exports, which has been undermined by non-participating countries like Israel and lax enforcement by European nations. Given these precedents, the author suggests that government-mandated export controls are unlikely to be an effective long-term solution for restricting access to dual-use cyber technologies, as capable actors will eventually develop similar capabilities regardless of restrictions.
