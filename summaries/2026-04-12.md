### Walmart-owned Flipkart, Amazon are squeezing India’s quick commerce startups - (TechCrunch)

https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/11/walmart-owned-flipkart-amazon-are-squeezing-indias-quick-commerce-startups/

# Summary

India's quick commerce market is experiencing intense competition as Walmart-owned Flipkart and Amazon aggressively expand their fast-delivery services, putting pressure on dedicated startups like Blinkit, Swiggy, and Zepto. Flipkart has crossed 800 dark stores (distribution centers) and plans to double that by end of 2026, while Amazon operates 330–370 stores. Flipkart is differentiating itself through aggressive pricing (23–24% discounts) and expansion into smaller towns beyond major metro areas, which currently account for most of the market's profitability.

The competitive squeeze is showing cracks in the sector, with analyst warnings that Swiggy's quick commerce business faces a "growth-versus-profitability deadlock" and stock prices of major players falling significantly. Industry experts predict the sector—no longer in a startup phase—will likely consolidate as large, well-capitalized players compete for customers in a discount-heavy market with limited differentiation. Quick commerce is currently viable in about 125 cities, with new stores taking 6–12 months to reach profitability.

### The Center Has a Bias - (Armin Ronacher's Thoughts and Writings)

https://lucumr.pocoo.org/2026/4/11/the-center-has-a-bias/

# Summary

Ronacher argues that discussions about new technologies like AI coding agents are polarized because the "center" position—held by people with genuine, nuanced views—is inherently biased toward engagement and experimentation rather than rejection or enthusiasm. While critics of these tools often raise legitimate concerns, their perspective frequently lacks the grounded, hands-on experience necessary for truly informed judgment. Conversely, enthusiastic adopters may suffer from overcommitment bias. The author contends that forming a measured opinion requires substantial time and engagement with the technology, which means the actual center inevitably appears to shift toward the adoption side behaviorally, even though this engagement doesn't equal endorsement.

The crucial insight is that serious criticism often comes from those who use the tools extensively—people who can identify specific failures and limitations precisely because they've invested time exploring them. This creates a paradox: the most informed critics can appear compromised to outsiders who haven't engaged with the technology, making it difficult to distinguish between genuine, nuanced skeptics and uncritical enthusiasts. Ronacher emphasizes that while some technology deserves outright resistance, understanding *why* requires sufficient contact with the thing itself.
