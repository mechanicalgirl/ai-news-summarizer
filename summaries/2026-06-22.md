### Deno Desktop - (Hacker News)

https://docs.deno.com/runtime/desktop/

# Summary

**Deno Desktop** is an upcoming feature (coming in Deno 2.9) that converts Deno projects—from simple TypeScript files to complex frameworks like Next.js—into self-contained desktop applications. Each compiled binary bundles your code, the Deno runtime, and a web rendering engine, creating redistributable apps for different platforms.

The tool addresses common desktop development tradeoffs by prioritizing small binaries (using the OS's native webview by default), full Node.js ecosystem compatibility, automatic framework detection, in-process backend-UI communication, cross-platform compilation from a single machine, and built-in auto-update capabilities. Developers can create a desktop app as simply as writing a basic HTTP server and running `deno desktop`, with support for multiple windows, native dialogs, menus, notifications, and DevTools integration.

### GLM 5.2 vs. Opus - (Hacker News)

https://techstackups.com/comparisons/glm-5.2-vs-opus/

# Summary

GLM-5.2, Z.ai's new open-weight model, was tested head-to-head against Claude Opus 4.8 in a challenging real-world task: building a 3D platformer game from scratch in raw WebGL. GLM-5.2 completed the task for $5.39 in 1 hour 10 minutes, while Opus finished in 33 minutes for approximately $21.92. Both models successfully built functional games with proper 3D engines, collision detection, and controls, demonstrating that GLM-5.2 is genuinely capable at a fraction of the cost.

However, Opus delivered a noticeably cleaner final product with correct textures, proper win conditions, and working hazards, while GLM-5.2's game had visible bugs including missing textures and incomplete functionality. The key difference was that Opus, being multimodal, could inspect screenshots of its work and fix issues before finishing, whereas GLM-5.2 could only verify through pixel-reading workarounds that missed visual problems. While the authors won't replace Opus for production work, they view GLM-5.2 as a valuable addition to their toolkit due to its affordability and open-source availability—advantages that make it immune to sudden discontinuation like proprietary models.

### Help I accidentally a wigglegram - (Hacker News)

https://lmao.center/blog/wiggle-accidents/

# Summary

A photographer discovered they had accidentally created hundreds of "wigglegrams" (stereo images made by looping similar frames together like GIFs) while taking multiple shots from slightly different angles. Rather than manually sorting through years of photos, they wrote a script using perceptual hashing—the same technique used by reverse image search engines—to automatically identify and extract sequences of similar images from their camera roll.

The script successfully produced hundreds of wigglegrams with varying results: some intentionally taken ones created convincing stereoscopic effects, while most accidental ones produced kinescopic "mini-movies" instead. The results ranged across diverse subjects including animals, design work, and sculptures, demonstrating that the technique works particularly well with unpredictable subjects like moving animals. The author shared the script on GitHub for others to use on their own photo libraries.

### Codex logging bug may write TBs to local SSDs - (Hacker News)

https://github.com/openai/codex/issues/28224

# Summary

OpenAI's Codex is generating excessive write volume to its SQLite feedback logging database, potentially writing ~640 TB per year and rapidly depleting SSD endurance. After just 21 days of uptime, one user's machine had written 37 TB, which extrapolates to consuming a typical consumer SSD's entire warranted lifespan (600 TBW) in less than a year.

The root cause is that the SQLite feedback log sink uses a global TRACE-level logging default, persisting all logs including low-value dependency noise, raw websocket/SSE payloads, and duplicated telemetry events. TRACE logs alone account for 70.7% of retained bytes, with OpenTelemetry mirror logs adding another 25.3%. The issue notes significant write amplification, with ~36,000 rows inserted every 15 seconds despite flat retention. The proposed fix involves narrowing default log filtering by disabling global TRACE logging, excluding noisy dependencies, storing only summaries instead of raw payloads, and implementing global database size caps.

### Apertus – Open Foundation Model for Sovereign AI - (Hacker News)

https://apertvs.ai/

# Summary

APERTUS is a fully open-source foundation AI model developed by the Swiss AI Initiative through a collaboration between EPFL, ETH Zurich, and CSCS. The model prioritizes transparency by making its training data, code, weights, and methods publicly available and reproducible. It is designed to be compliant with EU AI Act requirements, incorporating features like PII removal and memorization prevention, while delivering competitive performance comparable to leading open models at 8B and 70B parameter scales.

The initiative supports multilingual capabilities across 1000+ languages and emphasizes sovereign AI development. Recent releases include APERTUS Mini (a collection of 16 small language models) and fine-tuned variants for specific applications like translation services in Ticino, with ongoing research contributions presented at major conferences like ACL 2026.

### Munich 1991: The Roots of the Current AI Boom - (Hacker News)

https://people.idsia.ch/~juergen/ai-boom-roots-munich-1991.html

# Summary

In 1991, Jürgen Schmidhuber's lab at Technical University Munich published groundbreaking research that established virtually all core technologies powering today's AI boom, including the Transformer architecture (the "T" in ChatGPT), unsupervised pre-training (the "P"), neural network distillation, deep residual learning, and generative adversarial networks. These four techniques, published within just months of each other, form the foundation of modern Large Language Models and have become among the most cited AI papers of the past 30 years.

Despite laying the theoretical groundwork for a trillion-dollar industry while computing was millions of times more expensive than today, Munich's dominance in AI research declined over subsequent decades as commercial AI development shifted to the Pacific Rim. Schmidhuber emphasizes that these early innovations were designed with the ultimate goal of achieving Artificial General Intelligence in mind, incorporating concepts like adaptive world models, artificial curiosity, meta-learning, and recursive self-improvement—approaches he argues remain essential for AGI beyond current LLM-focused approaches.

### Memory Safe Inline Assembly - (Hacker News)

https://fil-c.org/inlineasm

# Summary

Fil-C, a memory-safe C/C++ compiler, is introducing support for safe inline assembly—a notoriously unsafe feature that can cause compiler miscompilations if used incorrectly. The article identifies legitimate use cases for inline assembly in real-world code, including preventing compiler optimizations (for constant-time crypto), executing CPU feature detection instructions (cpuid, xgetbv), accessing x87 floating-point functions, and implementing atomic operations. These use cases are widespread in critical libraries that most programs depend on.

To enable safe inline assembly while maintaining memory safety, Fil-C validates inline assembly at the compiler level by parsing both the assembly instructions and constraint strings, rejecting any code that contains memory accesses, control flow, or unrecognized side effects. The compiler ensures that all assembly effects are properly captured in the constraints—for example, if an instruction modifies a register or sets CPU flags, those changes must be declared in the constraint string. The author leveraged AI to implement the x86_64 assembly parser needed for this validation, making the feature feasible to develop.

### Everything is logarithms - (Hacker News)

https://alexkritchevsky.com/2026/05/25/everything-is-logarithms.html

# Summary

This article explores the concept of "baseless logarithms" as a way to better understand logarithmic operations. The author argues that standard logarithm notation obscures the fundamental question logarithms answer: "how many factors of b are in x?" By treating logarithms as abstract, unitless objects (log N) that only gain numerical meaning when divided by another logarithm (like log 2), the change-of-base formula becomes intuitive—similar to converting between measurement units. This baseless approach mirrors how geometric vectors work: just as vectors are fundamental geometric objects that gain coordinates through a choice of basis, logarithms are fundamental multiplicative objects that gain numerical values through a choice of base.

The author then demonstrates striking parallels between logarithms and vectors in coordinate systems. Both involve abstract geometric objects (vectors and baseless logarithms) that only acquire numerical meaning relative to a chosen reference frame (basis vectors and logarithm bases). The change-of-base formula for logarithms directly corresponds to coordinate transformations for vectors, and expressing values "in units" works identically in both frameworks. This deeper structural similarity reveals that logarithms and vectors are fundamentally analogous mathematical objects—both require an arbitrary choice of reference point to translate their abstract form into concrete numbers.
