### Humanoid Robot Actuators - (Hacker News)

https://www.firgelli.com/pages/humanoid-robot-actuators

# Summary

This article is a comprehensive engineering guide on actuators for humanoid robots, written by a chief engineer at Firgelli Automations. The core problem is that humanoid robots walking at ~84 steps per minute experience roughly 5,000 impacts per hour, with each step generating 2-3× body weight in shock force. Unlike static industrial applications, actuators must repeatedly absorb these dynamic impacts sub-millisecond-fast, requiring mechanical "back-drivability" to avoid catastrophic gearbox failure. Traditional self-locking actuators cannot handle this repeated shock cycling.

The article explains that bipedal robots have 10-50× worse energy efficiency than wheeled vehicles, making actuator mass a critical constraint—every gram compounds energy costs. The guide covers why all major humanoid manufacturers (Tesla, Boston Dynamics, Unitree) converged on rotary actuators for major joints, the importance of specific torque metrics (output per unit mass), and the fundamental engineering trade-offs between force, mass, durability, and thermal management that determine success in humanoid robot design.

### Using "underdrawings" for accurate text and numbers - (Hacker News)

https://samcollins.blog/underdrawings/

# Summary

Sam Collins describes an "underdrawing" technique for generating accurate text and numbers in AI-generated images. The method combines deterministic and generative tools by first creating a precise SVG or code-based layout with correct text, numbers, and positioning, then using that as an input image to a multimodal AI model (like Gemini 3.0 Pro) along with a detailed style prompt. This two-step approach overcomes the weakness of image generation models at rendering legible text while leveraging their strength in creating visually stunning results.

Collins demonstrates the technique with a game board example where standard image models fail to correctly number 50 stepping stones in sequence, but the underdrawing method succeeds. The approach is straightforward to implement—existing coding tools can automate both steps—though results may not be perfect every time.

### DeepClaude – Claude Code agent loop with DeepSeek V4 Pro - (Hacker News)

https://github.com/aattaran/deepclaude

# DeepClaude Summary

**DeepClaude** is an open-source tool that replaces Claude Code's underlying AI model with cheaper alternatives like DeepSeek V4 Pro, OpenRouter, or Fireworks AI while maintaining the same user interface and functionality. It achieves roughly 17x cost reduction—from $200/month with Anthropic to approximately $20-80/month depending on usage—by intercepting Claude Code's API calls and routing them through a local proxy server that supports multiple backends.

The tool preserves all of Claude Code's capabilities including file editing, bash execution, autonomous multi-step coding loops, and git operations. It supports live backend switching without restarting, allowing users to seamlessly toggle between DeepSeek (cheapest, with automatic context caching), OpenRouter (lowest US latency), Fireworks AI (fastest), or Anthropic's Claude Opus (for complex problems). Installation is straightforward (2 minutes), and it works across Windows, macOS, and Linux via simple command-line scripts.

### Discovering hard disk physical geometry through microbenchmarking (2019) - (Hacker News)

https://blog.stuffedcow.net/2019/09/hard-disk-geometry-microbenchmarking/

# Summary

This article explores techniques for measuring the physical geometry of hard disk drives through microbenchmarking without opening them up. The author explains that while early algorithms could characterize disk properties like platter count and head switch times, modern drives have become more complex with variable track and sector arrangements, rendering older methods obsolete. The article provides detailed background on how hard drives physically store data in platters, tracks, and sectors, and describes how timing specific sequences of disk reads can reveal information about rotation speed, sector positions, track boundaries, and seek times.

The author then presents findings from microbenchmarking 17 hard drives ranging from 45 MB (1989) to 5 TB (2015), revealing diverse track layouts across different eras and manufacturers. Key discoveries include that modern drives achieve track densities as small as 80 nanometers and that track arrangement varies significantly between old cylindrical designs and newer adjacent-track layouts. The article demonstrates that despite the challenges of precise measurement, microbenchmarking can effectively characterize physical disk geometry and identify defective sectors without destructive analysis.

### The 'Hidden' Costs of Great Abstractions - (Hacker News)

https://jdgr.net/the-hidden-costs-of-great-abstractions

# Summary

This article argues that as computing abstractions have become more powerful and accessible, developers have lost deep understanding of how systems actually work. While lower barriers to entry enabled faster development, the quality of software has suffered—much code is now slow and buggy. The author uses the analogy that not everything "good enough" is actually good—like cheap steel from Alibaba or Wonder Bread versus artisan sourdough.

The personal reflection reveals the author wrote this piece out of frustration during a difficult period: unemployed since July 2025 despite years of genuine technical expertise and passion for computing. With physical injuries limiting his work options and a family depending on him, he's struggled to remain competitive in a job market increasingly flooded with adequate-but-mediocre solutions generated by AI and less experienced developers. The article ultimately reflects his worry that deep technical knowledge—once essential and valued—may no longer guarantee meaningful employment.

### A Treasure Trove of Fossils Rewrites the Story of Early Life - (Hacker News)

https://www.quantamagazine.org/a-treasure-trove-of-cambrian-fossils-rewrites-the-story-of-early-life-20260501/

# Summary

Paleontologists in southern China have discovered the Huayuan biota, a remarkable collection of 8,681 exceptionally well-preserved Cambrian fossils spanning 153 species, with over half representing species new to science. This deposit, which postdates the Sinsk event (the first known mass extinction of the Phanerozoic Eon around 513.5 million years ago), provides crucial insights into how deep-ocean environments served as refuges for life during mass extinction and how modern animal groups evolved and diversified.

The discovery is particularly significant because it represents deep-sea ecosystems that paleontologists previously understood little about. Many fossils are nearly identical to those found in the famous Burgess Shale in Canada, suggesting global ocean connectivity in the Cambrian. The exceptional preservation of soft-bodied organisms reveals details about the origins of oceanic carbon cycling, the connections between ancient food webs, and the ecological complexity that existed in Earth's earliest complex ecosystems.

### A desktop made for one - (Hacker News)

https://isene.org/2026/05/Audience-of-One.html

# Summary

Geir Isene describes his journey of replacing nearly all off-the-shelf software on his computer with custom-built tools over the past 25 years. His current desktop environment consists of two layers: CHasm (written in x86_64 assembly for core system functions) and Fe₂O₃ (written in Rust for applications), replacing tools like vim, ranger, and mutt with his own versions like scribe, pointer, and kastrup. Notably, he rewrote his text editor in just 72 hours using Claude Code as his AI assistant, replacing 25 years of vim muscle memory with a customized alternative.

The author argues that modern tools like Rust and AI coding assistants have dramatically lowered the barrier to building personalized software environments, making it feasible to create a complete desktop tailored to one person in weekends rather than years. He emphasizes that his software is intentionally built only for himself, not for distribution, and highlights the freedom and efficiency that comes from eliminating the complexity of supporting multiple users and use cases. He concludes by encouraging others to consider building their own tools for at least one component of their workflow, even if they don't overhaul their entire desktop.
