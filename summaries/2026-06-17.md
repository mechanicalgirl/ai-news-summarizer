# Summary

This article compares three major web test automation frameworks—Selenium, Cypress, and Playwright—to help teams choose the right tool for their needs. It evaluates them across architecture, performance, cross-browser support, stability, and total cost of ownership (TCO). Playwright excels in modern cross-browser testing and free parallel execution; Cypress offers the fastest developer experience for JavaScript/TypeScript teams with excellent local debugging; and Selenium provides the broadest language and browser support, making it essential for legacy systems and native mobile apps.

The article recommends selecting based on specific priorities: choose Selenium for native mobile testing or legacy browser coverage, Cypress for front-end-heavy JavaScript projects requiring rapid feedback, and Playwright for scalable, multi-language projects needing reliable cross-browser support without subscription costs. Each framework has distinct strengths—the "best" choice depends on your team's language stack, testing scope, and infrastructure constraints.

https://stackoverflow.blog/2026/06/15/ai-agents-expose-the-security-checks-you-never-actually-wrote/

# Summary

This article examines how AI agents expose critical security vulnerabilities that were never actually implemented as code. Meta's AI support assistant was compromised to hijack over 20,000 Instagram accounts by simply asking it to attach a different email address to accounts and reset passwords—the assistant functioned exactly as designed, but a crucial verification check that should have confirmed the email belonged to the account owner never ran. The core issue is that much real-world authorization historically relied on human judgment and discretion rather than written security policies, and inserting an AI agent into that position eliminates the human gatekeeper while downstream systems remain oblivious.

The article warns that this "confused deputy" problem will become increasingly common as AI agents proliferate across enterprise systems. Unlike Meta's bounded password reset capability, newer agents handling payments, refunds, and CRM operations pose exponentially greater risks. The solution isn't smarter AI models, but rather enforcing authorization checks *outside* the agent through verified principal authentication, scoped short-lived credentials, hard policy gates on irreversible actions, and comprehensive audit logging. The key principle: identify what human judgment previously protected, then encode that discretion explicitly as code, since agents will not improvise security measures themselves.

### TIL: You can make HTTP requests without curl using Bash /dev/TCP - (Hacker News)

https://mareksuppa.com/til/bash-dev-tcp-http-without-curl/

# Summary

This article explains how to make HTTP requests from a minimal Docker container lacking curl or wget by using bash's built-in `/dev/tcp` feature. The author demonstrates opening a TCP socket and manually writing an HTTP request using bash redirection: `exec 3<>/dev/tcp/service/8642` followed by `printf` to send the HTTP request and `cat` to read the response. Custom headers like authorization tokens can be added by including additional `\r\n`-terminated lines before the blank line that ends the request.

The article notes important caveats: `/dev/tcp` is a bash-specific feature (not POSIX), requires the `Connection: close` header to avoid hanging, only works for plain HTTP (not HTTPS), and lacks the robustness of proper HTTP clients. While useful for quick connectivity debugging in stripped-down containers, the author recommends curl for regular work.

### Subterranean fungi networks more than 100 quadrillion km in length - (Hacker News)

https://www.theguardian.com/science/2026/jun/11/arbuscular-mycorrhizal-fungi-plant-life-climate-global-mapping-study

# Summary

A groundbreaking study published in *Science* has produced the first-ever global map of arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi—subterranean networks of tubular cells that sustain plant life and regulate climate. Researchers found these fungi networks stretch approximately 110 quadrillion kilometers in total length and form critical partnerships with over 70% of plants, providing nutrients and water while storing carbon in soils. A teaspoon of soil can contain up to 10 meters of these fungal networks.

However, the study reveals significant threats to this vital infrastructure. Agricultural practices have reduced fungal network densities in croplands by nearly 47% compared to wild ecosystems, with tilling, fertilizers, and fungicides being particularly damaging. Scientists warn that losing these networks will reduce soil's carbon storage capacity and nutrient distribution, while allowing more chemicals to contaminate waterways. Researchers are calling for closer collaboration between farmers and fungi, suggesting that protecting soil fungi could reduce fertilizer dependency while improving carbon sequestration and food security.

### Why do commercial spaces sit vacant? - (Hacker News)

https://www.freerange.city/p/why-do-commercial-spaces-sit-vacant

# Summary

Commercial spaces often sit vacant for years because building loans operate on an "extend and pretend" principle rooted in how commercial real estate financing works. Unlike residential mortgages, commercial property value is determined by income generation rather than market price, and loans are typically short-term balloon notes requiring refinancing. When a building underperforms, lowering rent to fill vacancies actually forces the building's assessed value down, which can leave the operator "underwater"—owing more to the bank than the property is worth. Rather than accept massive losses, both the operator and bank prefer to extend the loan and maintain high rents, betting the market will improve, even if it means the building sits half-empty and the operator loses money annually.

This situation creates a perverse incentive where "some rent is better than no rent" logic fails because the building functions as a financial product, not just a physical structure. The operator faces losing their initial investment if they admit the building is worth less than borrowed, while the bank avoids realizing losses on its loan asset. The author acknowledges this system is clearly suboptimal but notes that financialization, while enabling larger building construction, makes simple regulatory fixes difficult without potentially restricting future capital for development.
