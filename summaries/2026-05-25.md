### Didgeridoo playing as alternative treatment for obstructive sleep apnea(2006) - (Hacker News)

https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1360393/

# Summary

This randomized controlled trial tested whether playing the didgeridoo could improve symptoms of moderate obstructive sleep apnea syndrome (OSA). Twenty-five participants with moderate OSA were divided into two groups: one received four months of didgeridoo training and home practice, while the control group remained on a waiting list. The intervention group practiced an average of 5.9 days per week for about 25 minutes daily.

The results showed that didgeridoo playing produced significant improvements compared to the control group: daytime sleepiness decreased, the apnoea-hypopnoea index (a measure of breathing interruptions) improved, and partners reported less sleep disturbance. The researchers concluded that regular didgeridoo playing is an effective and well-tolerated alternative treatment for patients with moderate OSA, likely because the technique trains the muscles of the upper airways to prevent collapse during sleep.

### Migrating from Go to Rust - (Hacker News)

https://corrode.dev/learn/migration-guides/go-to-rust/

# Summary

This article compares Go and Rust for backend services, exploring why Go developers might consider migrating. While Go excels at ease of use with its lightweight runtime and clean toolchain, Rust provides stronger compile-time guarantees by encoding safety checks into the type system. Key differences include memory management (garbage collection vs. ownership), null safety (nil everywhere vs. Option<T>), and error handling (convention-based vs. exhaustive matching). The article notes that most Go-to-Rust migrations aren't driven by performance concerns, but rather by frustrations with Go's verbose error handling, nil pointer panics in production, and inability to catch data races at compile time.

The author, acknowledging his bias as a Rust consultant, positions the guide as an objective comparison for Go developers. He argues that Rust's steeper learning curve is offset by the compiler catching entire categories of bugs—particularly nil panics and data races—that would only surface in Go production environments. The toolchains are comparable, though Rust's cargo includes more built-in functionality, while Go typically requires third-party tools. Ultimately, the decision hinges on whether the upfront complexity of Rust's guarantees justifies eliminating runtime failures in your specific context.

### White Rabbit – sub-nanosecond synchronization for large distributed systems - (Hacker News)

https://ohwr.org/projects/white-rabbit/

# Summary

White Rabbit is an open-source synchronization technology that provides sub-nanosecond accuracy and picosecond precision for large distributed systems across distances up to 10 km. It enables precise time-tagging of measured data and deterministic data delivery while simultaneously using the same Ethernet-based network for gigabit-rate communication across thousands of nodes.

The project features fully open hardware, firmware, and software, with commercially produced multi-vendor hardware support. Developed at CERN, White Rabbit is actively maintained and expanding, with ongoing job opportunities for FPGA developers to work on evolving components like the White Rabbit switch v4 and the eRTM board, which achieves femtosecond-level precision.

### C constructs that still don't work in C++ - (Hacker News)

https://lospino.so/blog/c-constructs-that-still-dont-work-in-cpp/

# Summary

This 2026 article updates a 2019 survey of C constructs incompatible with C++, highlighting how the compatibility landscape has shifted with C++20, C++23, and C23 standards. The author emphasizes that precise language mode specification (e.g., C17 vs. C23, C++17 vs. C++20) is now essential when discussing C/C++ compatibility, as several previously incompatible features have been modified or aligned.

Key changes include: C++20 now supports designated initializers (though more restrictively than C), C23 aligned empty parameter lists with C++ semantics, and C++20 improved implicit object lifetime handling for malloc. However, important differences remain: C++ still lacks implicit void* conversion, restricts designated initializers to direct members in declaration order, and doesn't provide the full flexibility of C's initialization syntax. The practical advice centers on understanding that constructs may compile with casts but not be truly defined, distinguishing between storage allocation and object lifetime, and using appropriate C++ idioms (like `std::make_unique`) rather than C patterns like malloc.

### A fundamental principle of aeronautical engineering has been overturned - (Hacker News)

https://www.wired.com/story/a-fundamental-principle-of-aeronautical-engineering-has-been-overturned/

# Summary

For over 80 years, aeronautical engineering has operated on the principle that smoother surfaces reduce aerodynamic drag. However, researchers at Tohoku University have overturned this fundamental principle by demonstrating that applying distributed micro-roughness (DMR)—microscopic irregularities invisible to the naked eye—can reduce aerodynamic drag by up to 43.6 percent. Unlike the "shark skin" process that requires grooves aligned with airflow direction, DMR works through random surface irregularities that delay the transition from laminar to turbulent airflow, suppressing friction resistance itself.

The breakthrough was enabled by a new wind tunnel technology (a 1-meter magnetic support balance system) that eliminates airflow disruption from conventional support structures, allowing precise measurement of micro-scale drag changes. The research team used computational analysis to confirm that DMR's drag reduction comes primarily from suppressing wall friction rather than pressure resistance. With advantages including directional independence, no moving parts, low cost, and potential significant improvements in fuel efficiency and emissions for aircraft, this discovery could transform aeronautical and automotive design.
