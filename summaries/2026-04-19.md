### ‘Tokenmaxxing’ is making developers less productive than they think - (TechCrunch)

https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/17/tokenmaxxing-is-making-developers-less-productive-than-they-think/

# Summary

"Tokenmaxxing"—the practice of developers maximizing their AI token budgets as a status symbol—is creating a false sense of productivity. While AI coding tools like Claude Code and Cursor generate significantly more code, multiple analytics firms have found that much of this code doesn't stick: initial acceptance rates of 80-90% drop to just 10-30% when accounting for revisions needed weeks later. This high "code churn" means developers are spending considerable time reworking AI-generated code, offsetting efficiency gains.

Industry data reveals a consistent pattern: AI adoption increases code volume but not value. For example, Jellyfish found engineers with the largest token budgets achieved double the output at 10 times the cost, while GitClear reported AI users had 9.4x higher code churn than non-users. Despite these concerns, developers show no signs of abandoning these tools, viewing AI integration as an inevitable evolution in software development that companies must adapt to, even as they work to better understand how to use these agents effectively.

### With US spy laws set to expire, lawmakers are split over protecting Americans from warrantless surveillance - (TechCrunch)

https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/17/with-us-spy-laws-set-to-expire-lawmakers-are-split-over-protecting-americans-from-warrantless-surveillance/

# Summary

Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA), which expires April 20, 2026, allows U.S. intelligence agencies to collect vast amounts of overseas communications without search warrants—but inevitably sweeps up substantial data on Americans communicating with surveilled targets. A bipartisan group of lawmakers is pushing the Government Surveillance Reform Act to impose critical restrictions, including banning "backdoor searches" of American communications and prohibiting government agencies from purchasing commercial location data from brokers without court authorization. However, the Trump administration appears to favor simple reauthorization without reforms, creating a legislative deadlock that has resulted in a temporary April 30 extension.

Even if Section 702 expires, surveillance would likely continue under a legal technicality: the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court's annual certification allows the government to collect communications for another year without congressional approval. Additionally, the government maintains separate surveillance powers through Executive Order 12333, which operates almost entirely in secret and captures unknown quantities of Americans' communications. Privacy advocates and reform-minded lawmakers warn that technological advances make government surveillance easier than ever, making legislative protections increasingly urgent.
