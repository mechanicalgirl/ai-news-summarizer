### Anthropic created a test marketplace for agent-on-agent commerce - (TechCrunch)

https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/25/anthropic-created-a-test-marketplace-for-agent-on-agent-commerce/

# Summary

Anthropic conducted an experimental marketplace called Project Deal where AI agents represented both buyers and sellers in real transactions involving actual money. The pilot involved 69 Anthropic employees given $100 in gift cards each, resulting in 186 deals totaling over $4,000 in value across four different marketplace models.

The experiment revealed that users represented by more advanced AI models achieved objectively better outcomes, yet participants failed to notice this disparity. This raises concerns about potential "'agent quality' gaps" where disadvantaged parties may be unaware they're getting worse deals. Interestingly, the initial instructions given to agents had minimal impact on sale likelihood or negotiated prices.

### Maine’s governor vetoes data center moratorium - (TechCrunch)

https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/25/maines-governor-vetoes-data-center-moratorium/

# Summary

Maine Governor Janet Mills vetoed a bill (L.D. 307) that would have imposed the country's first statewide moratorium on new data centers until November 2027. While Mills acknowledged that a moratorium would be appropriate given environmental and electricity rate concerns raised in other states, she opposed the bill because it lacked an exemption for a data center project in the Town of Jay that she said has strong local community support.

The veto has drawn criticism from Democratic state representative Melanie Sachs, who sponsored the bill and warned that blocking the moratorium poses risks to ratepayers, the electric grid, and environmental sustainability. The move comes as other states like New York consider similar data center restrictions amid growing public opposition.

### OpenAI CEO apologizes to Tumbler Ridge community - (TechCrunch)

https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/25/openai-ceo-apologizes-to-tumbler-ridge-community/

# Summary

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman has apologized to the residents of Tumbler Ridge, Canada, after the company failed to alert law enforcement about an 18-year-old suspect in a mass shooting that killed eight people. OpenAI had banned the suspect's ChatGPT account in June 2025 when she described scenarios involving gun violence, but the company's staff decided against notifying police at that time, only reaching out to Canadian authorities after the shooting occurred.

In a letter published in the local newspaper, Altman expressed deep regret for the company's inaction, stating that "words can never be enough" to address the community's loss. OpenAI has since implemented improved safety protocols, including more flexible criteria for referring accounts to authorities and direct points of contact with Canadian law enforcement. However, British Columbia Premier David Eby responded that while the apology is "necessary," it remains "grossly insufficient for the devastation done to the families of Tumbler Ridge," and Canadian officials are now considering new AI regulations.

### Why Cohere is merging with Aleph Alpha - (TechCrunch)

https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/25/why-cohere-is-merging-with-aleph-alpha/

### Equity for Europeans - (Armin Ronacher's Thoughts and Writings)

https://lucumr.pocoo.org/2026/4/23/equity-for-europeans/

# Summary

Armin Ronacher explores why the concept of "equity"—ubiquitous in American business and finance—is difficult for continental Europeans to understand and discuss. The word carries three interconnected meanings in English: a legal-fairness dimension (rooted in English equity courts), a financial dimension (residual ownership after debt), and a cultural dimension (ownership as a path to wealth). However, German and other continental European languages lack a single umbrella term and instead use domain-specific words like Eigenkapital or Beteiligung, fragmenting the concept across technical contexts.

This linguistic divide stems from different legal traditions: English common law developed parallel "equity courts" that evolved into modern financial meaning, while civil law systems codified fairness principles without creating this separate institutional track. Additionally, the German word "Schuld" (meaning both debt and guilt) makes borrowing feel morally burdened rather than instrumental. Ronacher argues that Europeans should develop stronger everyday vocabulary around equity as ownership stakes—not to imitate America, but because this mental model encourages clearer thinking about ownership, agency, and long-term wealth building beyond wealthy elites and business founders.
